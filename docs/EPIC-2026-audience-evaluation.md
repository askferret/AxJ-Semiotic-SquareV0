## 2. MESSAGING & COPY ANALYSIS

### Strengths

**Landing Copy (Page Header):**
> "Map story tensions and find voices in the gaps. Paste a story pitch or field notes; the tool surfaces oppositions and contradictions, then suggests **source types to look for**."

- ✓ **Concrete and action-oriented.** Not "explore semantic structures"—users know immediately what to do.
- ✓ **Audience-appropriate terminology.** "Story tensions," "field notes," "oppositions," "contradictions" are in-domain for journalists and social scientists.
- ✓ **Emphasis on **people**, not concepts.** "Source types to look for" centers the goal: finding voices. This is journalist-friendly.

**"How It Works" Section:**
- ✓ Icons + brief explanations are scannable.
- ✓ Example (pro-development vs. protect character) shows journalistic use case.
- ✓ Clear distinction between opposition vs. contradiction.
- ✓ Mentions use cases (pitch development, field notes, teaching, editorial meetings)—broad applicability signal.

**Tagline:**
> "Find sources in between"

- ✓ **Memorable and statement-like.** Works as both tagline and mission statement.
- ✓ **Anti-binary message is clear.** Speaks directly to both journalism reform and social science critique of dichotomous thinking.
- ✓ **Accessible but not reductive.** Doesn't over-simplify the method.

### Gaps & Opportunities

**1. Missing: Why This Matters in an AI Era** (Critical for your audience)

Current copy is neutral on context. For EPIC attendees—especially **tech-focused journalists and AI-critical social scientists**—the **problem statement** should address:

- **Polarization amplification:** How algorithmic feeds and AI-generated summaries flatten complexity into binaries
- **Journalistic need:** How AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude) make binary framing faster and more seductive—and why you need this *because* of AI, not despite it
- **Research relevance:** How social scientists studying AI governance, content moderation, and platform cultures need tools to map stakeholder oppositions (not as binaries, but as contradictions)

**Suggested addition to landing copy:**
> "As AI-driven content amplifies polarized framing, finding diverse sources becomes urgent. Semiotic Square helps journalists and researchers map story tensions beyond binaries—to discover stakeholders, arguments, and voices that algorithms often miss."

This anchors the tool in the **problems your audience already cares about.**

---

**2. Missing: The AI Enabling Angle** (Moderate for your audience)

The app uses AI (Claude/Gemini) but doesn't explain **why** or **what advantage** that brings. For an **AI-literate audience**, this is a key pitch:

**Current framing:** "Generate Semiotic Square" (functional, but minimal)

**Opportunity:**
- Explain that AI enables **rapid structuring** of complex field notes (ethnographers spend hours coding; AI does structural analysis in seconds)
- Highlight that AI finds **non-obvious contradictions** (what an editorial meeting might miss)
- Frame it as **augmentation, not replacement:** AI suggests the structure; journalists/researchers refine, edit, decide

**Suggested UI enhancement:**
In the "How it works" section or results, add brief note:
> "The AI identifies semantic structure from your text. Your editorial judgment shapes the sources you seek."

This signals:
- ✓ AI as a generative tool, not an oracle
- ✓ Human-in-the-loop design (appeals to both journalist and researcher ethics)
- ✓ Respects domain expertise of users

---

**3. Underexplained: Model Choice** (Technical, but matters for researchers)

The app offers Claude Sonnet 4.6 vs. Gemini Flash 3. For **researchers**, this choice has implications:
- Reproducibility (different models → different outputs)
- Cost and latency trade-offs
- Bias/reasoning differences

**Current framing:** Simple dropdown, no guidance.

**Opportunity:**
> "Choose your model: Claude Sonnet 4.6 (richer reasoning, slower) vs. Gemini Flash 3 (fast, lightweight). Experiment to see which fits your workflow."

This is a research-friendly framing that acknowledges epistemic choice.

---

**4. Copy Tone: Appropriately Academic, But Could Warm Slightly**

**Current tone:** Professional, technical, somewhat formal.

**Assessment:**
- ✓ Appropriate for EPIC, where "semiotic square" is expected vocabulary
- ⚠️ May feel overly academic to **product/policy/business practitioners** who adapt this method beyond journalism
- ⚠️ Phrases like "semantic structure," "contradictions," and "field notes" are correct but could feel gatekeeping to non-academics

**Example:** "Inherited practices and identities rooted in long-standing relationships with natural ecosystems and place" (from the fishing sample)—this is excellent descriptive writing, but very academic in tone.

**Suggested tone shift (minor):** Add one more conversational layer to scaffolding. Example:

> **Current:** "The negation of each pole — not the other pole, but what contradicts it (e.g. Alienation contradicts Belonging)."
> **Alternative:** "What pushes back against each pole—not the opposite position, but what contradicts it. Example: someone alienated from a group contradicts the idea of 'belonging,' but isn't 'excluded' either."

This gives both expert and novice readers a handhold.

## 3. AUDIENCE-SPECIFIC CONSIDERATIONS

### For **Social Scientists / Ethnographers**

**What they'll love:**
- ✓ Method is grounded in real anthropological/linguistic tradition (Greimas)
- ✓ "Guided worksheet" mode respects iterative research process
- ✓ Source prompts encourage *relational thinking*—finding voices in tension, not lists

**What might confuse them:**
- ⚠️ AI's role in "discovering" structure feels too automated for serious ethnographic work
- ⚠️ No mention of **positionality** (whose perspective is the AI centering? What biases in the prompt?)
- ⚠️ Limited guidance on how to *validate* the AI's opposition/contradiction framing

**Recommendation:** Add a brief epistemic note:
> "The AI suggests structure based on your input and its training. Use this as a springboard for discussion, not a final analysis. Are the oppositions accurate for your community? What's the AI missing?"

---

### For **Tech-Focused Journalists / Media Professionals**

**What they'll love:**
- ✓ "Find sources in between" speaks to their beat (algorithms create filter bubbles; tool counters that)
- ✓ Fast workflow (Quick mode) respects deadline pressure
- ✓ Export PDF is practical

**What might feel underdeveloped:**
- ⚠️ No explicit connection to **AI/content moderation coverage** (hot beat)
- ⚠️ Limited guidance on how to use this for *reporting on AI companies/platforms* (e.g., mapping stakeholder positions in a policy story)
- ⚠️ No mention of how this tool could be adapted for **analyzing AI-generated content, recommendation systems, or platform governance**

**Recommendation:** Add an "Advanced use case" in help section:
> **Mapping AI stories:** Use this tool to structure complex stakeholder positions in technology policy, platform governance, or content moderation stories. Map positions of platforms, advocates, researchers, and affected communities—not just pro/con."

---

### For **Policy / Consulting / Product Practitioners** (Secondary but present at EPIC)

**Potential (if they adapt the tool):**
- ✓ "Opposition" and "contradiction" map to stakeholder analysis
- ✓ "Sources in between" = finding unlikely allies or bridge-builders
- ✓ Structure could work for organizational change, product positioning

**What's missing:**
- ⚠️ No language suggesting adaptability beyond journalism
- ⚠️ No case studies or examples from product/policy domains
- ⚠️ "Sources" framing is journalism-specific (in policy, it's "stakeholders," "constituencies," "actors")

**Recommendation (Minor):** In the "How it works" / use cases, consider adding:
> "While built for journalism, this method works for any complex situation with stakeholders in tension: organizational change, product design, policy advocacy, research communities."

## 4. STRENGTH: Accessibility & Intellectual Honesty

**Key phrase that impressed me:**
> "Journalist-adapted method, not a strict Greimas semiotic square."

This is **epistemically honest**. It says:
- ✓ We know the original theory
- ✓ We adapted it *deliberately* for another field
- ✓ We're not claiming to be pure semiotics

This builds credibility with critical audiences (academics, researchers, journalists trained in media studies).

## 7. Final Assessment

### For EPIC 2026 Conference Context

**The MVP is **excellent as a provocation and research probe.**

**Strengths:**
- Will intrigue social scientists (method transparency, epistemic honesty)
- Will resonate with journalists (practical, faster than manual stakeholder mapping)

2. **One note on human-in-the-loop** ("AI suggests; you decide")
3. **One use-case example from social science or policy** (beyond journalism)

These three additions will make the tool feel **less niche** and more like a **general-purpose method for mapping complexity.**

**Recommendation:** MVP messaging is **strong for the core audience** (journalists + social scientists). To reach secondary audiences (policy, product, consulting practitioners) and to emphasize the **AI-era relevance**, add 3-4 brief clarifications around **why this matters now**, **how AI enables it**, and **where else it might apply**.

The tool's strength is its **intellectual honesty and practical design**. The copy reflects that. A small amount of additional framing will make it sing at EPIC.
